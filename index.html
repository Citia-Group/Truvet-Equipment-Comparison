What these four vendors do NOT cover

The vendor comparison answers "which machine," not "can we pass an HSA / PIC-S inspection." A GMP solid-dose licence depends on equipment, instrumentation, facility infrastructure, and a documentation system working as one. The 16 gaps below are everything outside the four quotations that still has to be sourced, built, or written before the first PPQ batch.

Severity key: Critical blocks licensing or batch release · High required, has lead time, plan now · Medium needed but lower risk. Click any card for the rationale and what to ask.

Bucket A — Equipment & Instrumentation Gaps
A1
Validated CIP / WIP cleaning across the line
Only the coater WIP is quoted — granulation, press, capsule, blister are not
Critical▼

Cleaning validation needs a defined, repeatable cleaning method for every product-contact surface, not just the coating drum. The granulator bowl, FBD, bin blender, press turret/feed frame, and capsule dosing parts all need either an automated wash or a documented, validatable manual SOP with swab-accessible geometry.

Ask all vendorsRecommended cleaning procedure per machine, product-contact Ra values, and whether any CIP/WIP option exists. Confirm no dead legs or inaccessible crevices on product-contact paths.
A2
Potent-API containment / OEB rating
No containment or OEB classification in any quote
High▼

If any current or future product is a potent compound, open handling at dispensing, granulation, and compression creates an operator-exposure and cross-contamination risk. Containment (closed transfers, isolators, or split butterfly valves) is far cheaper designed in now than retrofitted.

Decide internally, then askDefine the highest OEB band the facility must handle. Then ask each vendor what containment options exist for the relevant machines.
A3
Automated tablet visual inspection
Blister camera ≠ whole-tablet defect inspection
Medium▼

The blister-line camera checks pocket fill and print, not tablet cosmetic defects (capping, chipping, spots). For higher volumes a standalone tablet inspection unit may be needed; at launch volumes documented manual AQL sampling can bridge it.

Ask press vendorWhether a tablet sorter/inspection add-on is available and its cost — decide build-now vs manual-AQL-for-now.
A4
Real-time tablet weight / force feedback control
Confirm closed-loop, not just a reject gate
High▼

Item 59 in the checklist asks about weight variation, but in-process control is stronger than end-of-line rejection: the press should measure compression force per tablet and auto-correct fill depth. Confirm whether the HGZ-26D does closed-loop control or only rejects out-of-spec tablets.

Ask Rich Packing (press)Does the press auto-adjust fill on force drift, or only reject? Is force logged per batch to the audit trail?
A5
Stability chambers (ICH Q1A(R2))
~$80K–200K · Memmert / Binder / Weiss · not in any quote
High▼

Registration and shelf-life claims require ICH stability data: long-term (25°C/60%RH), intermediate (30°C/65%), accelerated (40°C/75%), plus Zone IVb (30°C/75%) for tropical markets. Chambers have long lead times and must run for months before you have data — order early.

Separate procurementSource from Memmert, Binder, or Weiss. Specify mapping/qualification and 21 CFR Part 11-capable data logging.
A6
Serialisation / aggregation (DSCSA, EU FMD)
Needed for US/EU export — printing + vision + data
High▼

Exporting to the US (DSCSA) or EU (FMD) requires unit-level serialisation: print, verify, and aggregate codes to case/pallet with a data backbone reporting to authorities. This bolts onto the cartoning line and is easiest specified before the cartoner is built.

Ask Hualian (cartoner)Whether a serialisation module (Antares, Laetus, or Optel) can integrate on the ZHJ-150K cartoner, and what data interface it exposes.
A7
QC laboratory
~$500K–1.5M · HPLC, dissolution, KF, etc. + LIMS · entirely separate
Critical▼

No batch can be released without QC testing. A solid-dose lab needs HPLC, dissolution apparatus, disintegration, hardness/friability, Karl Fischer, balances, and a LIMS or validated spreadsheet system — all qualified and all Part 11 relevant. This is a major separate capital line frequently underestimated.

Not a vendor questionBuild a standalone QC lab capital plan and headcount. This is independent of the four equipment vendors.
A8
Calibration & metrology management
Every instrument needs traceable, scheduled calibration
High▼

Each temperature probe, pressure gauge, balance, and flow meter on the line needs initial and periodic calibration traceable to national standards, with a managed schedule. Inspectors check this early. Decide on in-house metrology vs an accredited (CNAS/ILAC) service contract.

Ask all vendors + plan internallyRequest the instrument list per machine with calibration points; set up a calibration management programme before commissioning.
Bucket B — Facility Infrastructure (none of the four vendors supply this)
B1
HVAC & cleanroom classification
PharmOut scope — Grade C/D, pressure cascades, AHUs
Critical▼

Solid-dose processing runs in classified space (typically Grade C/D) with controlled temperature, humidity, pressure cascades, and HEPA filtration. This is the single largest facility cost and the backbone of contamination control — it sits with PharmOut, not the equipment vendors.

PharmOut design itemConfirm classification per room, pressure scheme, and air-change rates are in the facility design package.
B2
Purified Water generation & distribution loop
PW system + hot/cold recirculating loop, validated
Critical▼

Coating, granulation binder solution, and cleaning all need Purified Water meeting pharmacopoeia spec, produced and distributed through a sanitisable recirculating loop and validated over a multi-month programme. Long lead time and long qualification — start early.

Separate utility projectSpecify PW generation capacity, loop design, and a 3-phase qualification plan with the facility team.
B3
Oil-free compressed air (ISO 8573-1)
Product-contact air must be clean & dry to spec
High▼

Compressed air touches product (fluidisation, conveying, blowing). It must be oil-free and meet ISO 8573-1 limits for particles, water, and oil, with the testing to prove it. Several vendor machines assume a clean dry air supply that the facility has to provide.

Facility utility + ask vendorsGet each machine's air quality/pressure/flow requirement; size an oil-free compressor + dryer + filtration train to ISO 8573-1.
B4
Multi-product facility flow & cross-contamination control
Material/personnel flow, airlocks, segregation
High▼

A multi-product plant must prevent mix-ups and cross-contamination through one-directional material and personnel flow, airlocks, gowning, and dedicated or validated-shared equipment. This is a facility-design and procedural matter that frames how the equipment is laid out.

PharmOut design itemConfirm flow diagrams, airlock strategy, and segregation approach are documented in the design.
Bucket C — Regulatory & Documentation Systems (you must build these)
C1
Pharmaceutical Quality System (ICH Q10 / PIC-S)
The QMS the whole licence sits on
Critical▼

HSA expects a functioning PQS aligned to PIC-S GMP and ICH Q10: change control, deviations, CAPA, document control, batch release by a qualified person, management review. No amount of good equipment substitutes for this. It must exist and be operating before licensing.

Not a vendor questionStand up the QMS (in-house or consultant-built) on its own timeline — it gates the licence.
C2
Technology transfer + 3 PPQ batches
Process validation before commercial release
High▼

Each product needs documented technology transfer and process validation — conventionally three consecutive successful Process Performance Qualification batches at commercial scale — before routine release. This depends on equipment being qualified first, so it sits at the end of the commissioning chain.

Internal protocolWrite tech-transfer and PPQ protocols per product; schedule after IQ/OQ/PQ of the line.
C3
Computer System Validation Master Plan
First document inspectors ask for on computerised systems
Critical▼

Every PLC/HMI, the SCADA/MES layer, LIMS, and stability-chamber logger is a computerised system needing validation under a CSV Master Plan and a GAMP 5 risk-based approach. This is the document an inspector requests first when reviewing data integrity — see the MES/SCADA tab for the system architecture it governs.

Internal — links to MES/SCADA tabAuthor a CSV Master Plan covering all computerised systems and their Part 11 controls.
C4
Analytical method validation (ICH Q2(R2))
Test methods must be validated, not just available
High▼

Assay, dissolution, impurity, and content-uniformity methods must be validated to ICH Q2(R2) — specificity, accuracy, precision, linearity, range — and, for transferred compendial methods, verified on your instruments. This pairs with the QC lab (A7) and gates batch release.

QC lab workstreamPlan method validation/verification per product alongside QC lab build-out.

Why build the data layer internally

The four vendors deliver machine-level control (PLC + HMI). They do not deliver the layer that ties machines together into electronic batch records, a process historian, and inspection-ready data integrity. That layer — SCADA + MES — is the part HSA scrutinises most under Part 11, and it is the part that becomes a reusable asset across every future Citia site. Outsourcing it per-site rebuilds the same thing repeatedly; building it once internally turns it into transferable infrastructure.

The principle matches the rest of the business: own the system, not just the product. A validated SCADA/MES platform with internal capability is a moat — it is hard to replicate, it compounds across facilities, and it keeps data-integrity control in-house rather than renting it.

Replication across Citia Group

The first site carries the full design, validation, and knowledge-transfer cost. A second or third facility reuses the validated platform, templates, and in-house team — replication runs an estimated 30–40% of first-site cost. That is the entire economic argument: outsourcing pays the full price every time; building once converts the spend into a transferable, compounding asset.

This only pencils out if there will be ≥2 more facilities. If TruVet SG is genuinely a one-site endeavour, the build-vs-outsource maths is much closer and outsourcing the integration deserves serious weight.

⚠ Key MES/SCADA risks

  • OPC-UA availability per machine is the make-or-break technical dependency — confirm it on-site for every Mandatory/Required machine before committing to the in-house build path.
  • GAMP Category 4 (configured) vs Category 5 (bespoke) drives validation effort. Off-the-shelf Ignition/Sepasoft configuration keeps it at Cat 4; heavy custom code pushes toward Cat 5 and far more validation.
  • Break-even depends on ≥2 further facilities materialising. Re-test this assumption against the actual roll-out plan.
  • Validation adds 4–6 months before first commercial batch — sequence the CSV programme so it does not become the critical path after equipment arrives.
