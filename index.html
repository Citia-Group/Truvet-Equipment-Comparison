⬡ Recommendation: Canaan — with targeted negotiation

For Truvet SG as a GMP-certified, HSA-regulated pharmaceutical manufacturing facility targeting an institutional trade sale or SGX IPO, the equipment selection decision is not purely a price-per-machine exercise. It is a regulatory infrastructure decision.

Canaan is the correct vendor for the core granulation and packaging line. The FDA 21 CFR Part 11 compliance — covering electronic signatures, audit trails, batch tracing — is not cosmetic: it is the minimum electronic system requirement that will be scrutinised by HSA during GMP inspection, and by any acquirer conducting technical due diligence. Urban does not document this capability in its quotations.

However, Canaan's granulation line has a significant capacity design problem: the LHS75 High Shear Mixer produces 15–30 kg batches, while the downstream Film Coater (BGX75) handles 20–60 kg and the Roller Compactor handles up to 80 kg/h. You will require 2–3 HSM cycles per coater/compactor cycle, building in a structural bottleneck from day one. This must be raised and resolved with Canaan — either by upgrading to the LHS150 or equivalent, or by accepting this as a phase-1 throughput limitation.

Urban's capsule filling machine (NJP-800C at $26K) is the only category where Urban is clearly superior on both throughput and completeness — twice the speed, polisher and metal detector included, at 40% higher price. Consider sourcing this from Urban separately if Canaan's NJP-400C is inadequate for projected capsule volumes.

A  |  Equipment Gaps — Missing From Both Proposals 8 items
A1
Validated CIP/WIP Cleaning System
Cross-contamination prevention between GS-441524, pimobendan, EIDD-1931, Flavaclovir
Critical ▼

Canaan lists WIP as an optional upgrade on the HSM and Film Coater. Urban mentions nothing. For a multi-product facility sharing equipment across four distinct APIs, validated cleaning between products is mandatory under 21 CFR 211.67 and EU GMP Chapter 5. Validated cleaning requires documented procedures, defined residual API acceptance limits (0.1% of minimum therapeutic dose, 10 ppm, or visually clean — strictest applies), and analytical verification by swab and rinse sampling.

Equipment must be designed for cleaning validation: no dead legs in pipework, surface finish Ra ≤ 0.8μm on all product contact surfaces, full drainability, and validated CIP spray coverage. These are design specifications, not afterthoughts.

Ask both vendors at factory visitIs your WIP/CIP system designed to achieve validated residual API acceptance limits per ASTM E3106? Can you provide a Cleaning Validation Protocol template? What is the surface finish Ra on all product contact parts?
A2
Containment for Potent APIs
Pimobendan (narrow therapeutic index), EIDD-1931 (nucleoside analogue)
Critical ▼

Neither vendor has addressed Occupational Exposure Limits (OELs) for your API portfolio. For any compound below 1–10 μg/m³ OEL — which pimobendan and EIDD-1931 likely approach — you require either closed RABS (Restricted Access Barrier System) or isolator technology at weighing and dispensing operations. At minimum, dedicated LAF booths with HEPA filtration are required.

This is an entirely absent line item. Containment capability must be engineered into room design and equipment specification simultaneously — it cannot be retrofitted after commissioning without major disruption.

Ask both vendorsCan your dispensing and granulation equipment be configured or enclosed to meet OEB 3/4 containment? What is your experience with potent API handling lines?
A3
Automated 100% Tablet/Capsule Visual Inspection
Pre-blister camera inspection of finished tablets and capsules
High ▼

Neither proposal includes automated visual inspection of tablets/capsules before they enter the blister packer. Urban's blister line camera system only inspects after packing. FDA 21 CFR 211.84 and increasing EMA practice require 100% inspection for defects — broken tablets, chips, discoloration, size variation. Camera-based 100% inspection is the baseline expectation during regulated market inspections for solid oral dosage.

Ask both vendorsDo you supply or integrate with a tablet/capsule camera inspection system between the tablet press/capsule filler and the blister packer? Which systems (Sensum, Antares Vision, Syntegon) have you integrated with?
A4
Real-Time Weight Checking on Tablet Press
Automated statistical process control for tablet weight variation
High ▼

FDA 21 CFR 211.110 requires in-process sampling and testing for tablet weight variation at defined intervals. Neither Canaan's GZPK-40 nor Urban's ZPTX340-24D includes a real-time automatic weight rejection system in the base configuration. Modern GMP compliance expects either an integrated auto-weight sampling system on the press itself, or a dedicated tablet weight checker with statistical process control and Part 11-compliant data logging. A metal detector for tablets (listed as option by both) is also not confirmed as included.

Ask both vendorsDoes the tablet press have integrated real-time weight control and auto-rejection? If not, which in-line tablet weight checker systems (Manesty, Kraemer) do you integrate with, and how does weight data feed into the batch record?
A5
ICH Q1A(R2) Stability Storage Chambers
Required for all regulatory submissions — NADA (FDA), MAA (EMA), TGA registration
Critical ▼

Without ICH-compliant stability data you cannot submit a New Animal Drug Application to FDA, a Marketing Authorisation Application to EMA, or register with TGA. ICH Q1A(R2) requires stability studies at 25°C/60% RH (long-term) and 40°C/75% RH (accelerated). Chambers require validated temperature and humidity uniformity, electronic monitoring integrated with Part 11 system, backup power, and alarm systems. This is absent from both proposals and represents a substantial separate capital line item ($80K–$200K).

Not a vendor questionSource separately from stability chamber specialists (Memmert, Binder, Weiss Technik). Confirm with your regulatory team which products require zone-specific stability conditions for your target markets.
A6
Serialisation / Track-and-Trace on Packaging Line
US DSCSA + EU FMD requirements for unit-level 2D DataMatrix coding
High ▼

US DSCSA (Drug Supply Chain Security Act) requires unit-level serialisation on all finished pharmaceutical products sold in the US. EU FMD (Falsified Medicines Directive) applies equivalent requirements in Europe. Urban's laser coder can print a 2D DataMatrix code, but serialisation requires integration with serialisation management software — generating unique serial numbers, managing aggregation (blister → carton → shipper), and reporting to DSCSA data exchange networks and the EU Hub. This is a software and integration requirement, not just a printer.

Ask both vendorsCan your blister packer and cartoner accept a serialisation module (e.g. Antares Vision, Laetus, Optel)? Do you have reference customers with fully serialised packaging lines? What is the mechanical interface specification for adding a serialisation unit?
A7
QC Laboratory Equipment
HPLC, dissolution, hardness, friability, KF titration, particle size — entirely absent
Critical ▼

A GMP manufacturer cannot release a single batch without documented analytical testing. The QC laboratory is a separate capital budget item of $500K–$1.5M for a fully equipped laboratory supporting four product lines. Required at minimum: HPLC/UPLC for assay and related substances, USP Type II dissolution apparatus, tablet hardness tester (automated with SPC), friability tester, disintegration apparatus, calibrated analytical balances (0.1 mg readability), Karl Fischer titration for moisture, pH meter, and laser diffraction particle size analyser. Not a vendor questionBudget separately. Source HPLC from Waters or Agilent (US/EU registration credibility). Plan LIMS (LabWare, STARLIMS) as an integrated system alongside your manufacturing MES.

B2
Purified Water Generation and Distribution System
RO+EDI generation, recirculating loop, USP/EP continuous monitoring
Critical ▼

Canaan offers optional 300L PW storage tanks; Urban offers nothing. Both are missing the PW generation and distribution infrastructure. GMP requires: validated generation (Reverse Osmosis + Electrodeionisation), distribution in a recirculating loop (prevents microbial growth in standing water), and continuous monitoring for conductivity (≤ 4.3 μS/cm at 20°C per USP/EP), TOC (≤ 500 ppb), microbial count (<100 CFU/mL), and endotoxin for highly purified water. A validated sanitisation procedure (hot water, ozone, or chemical) is required. The PW system is typically $150K–$400K depending on capacity and loop length.

Ask both vendorsWhat are the inlet water quality specifications for your WIP/CIP systems? Can your equipment connect to a third-party validated purified water distribution loop? What is the required water flow rate and pressure at point of use?
B3
Oil-Free Compressed Air System
ISO 8573-1 Class 1 quality; point-of-use filtration; validated dew point monitoring
Critical ▼

Both vendors list compressed air pressure requirements (0.4–0.6 MPa) but neither supplies the infrastructure. GMP mandates oil-free compressors, point-of-use filtration (1μm particulate → 0.01μm coalescence → activated carbon → 0.01μm sterile), and validated air quality per ISO 8573-1: Class 1 for particulates and oil, Class 2 for water. Validated dew point monitoring and calibrated pressure gauges at each use point are required. Estimated facility cost: $80K–$200K depending on system scale. Must be designed into the facility layout from day one — compressed air ring main routing affects room layouts.

Ask both vendorsWhat compressed air quality (ISO 8573-1 class) does each machine require at the point of use? Can you provide the instrument air quality specification in writing for inclusion in our utilities qualification protocol?
B4
Multi-Product Containment Infrastructure
Airlock design, dedicated air handling per suite, physical product segregation
High ▼

Physical separation between products is a GMP requirement, not just a procedural one. For a facility manufacturing GS-441524, pimobendan, EIDD-1931, and Flavaclovir on shared equipment, cross-contamination risk must be controlled at the facility design level: separate air handling per suite (no shared return air between different product manufacturing areas unless HEPA-filtered), airlocks between manufacturing suites, dedicated utensils and equipment per product or validated cleaning. This directly governs how many IBC bins, how many granulation campaign sequences, and what equipment sharing is permissible. Raise with PharmOut now — it affects floor plan design before construction begins.

C1
Pharmaceutical Quality System — ICH Q10
CAPA, change management, product quality monitoring, management review
Critical ▼

ICH Q10 requires a documented Pharmaceutical Quality System covering: product realisation, process performance and product quality monitoring, CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action), change management, and management review. This framework must be in place before any GMP inspection and is assessed independently of physical equipment. Neither vendor addresses this — it is an organisational infrastructure build that requires a qualified QA Director and a QMS software platform (Veeva Vault, MasterControl, or equivalent). Plan 12–18 months for implementation and internal qualification.

C2
Technology Transfer Protocols — Scale-Up Documentation
ICH Q8/Q11 process design; CPPs and CQAs defined per product
Critical ▼

For each product (GS-441524, pimobendan, EIDD-1931, Flavaclovir), formal technology transfer from development to commercial GMP scale is required: batch formula and manufacturing instruction translation, Critical Quality Attribute (CQA) identification, Critical Process Parameter (CPP) definition per ICH Q8/Q11, development history and risk assessments, and Process Analytical Technology strategy where applicable. This generates the technical dossier sections for regulatory submissions (Module 3 in CTD format for NADA/MAA). Requires pharmaceutical development scientists — not available from any equipment vendor.

C3
Computer System Validation Master Plan
GAMP 5 system inventory; validation approach per system; periodic review schedule
High ▼

Machine-level Part 11 compliance is necessary but not sufficient. A facility-level CSV Master Plan must enumerate every computerised system, classify each by GAMP 5 category (1–5), and define the validation approach, documentation requirements, and periodic review schedule. This covers manufacturing equipment PLCs, MES/SCADA, LIMS, QMS software, ERP, building management systems, and environmental monitoring. The CSV Master Plan is typically the first document an FDA or EMA inspector requests when reviewing computerised systems compliance — its absence signals systemic regulatory immaturity.

Ask both vendorsCan you provide a GAMP 5 software category classification for each machine's control system? Do you supply the CSV documentation package (URS, FS, configuration spec, IQ/OQ protocols) as a standard or optional deliverable? What is the cost?
C4
Technology Transfer — Analytical Method Validation
ICH Q2(R2); stability-indicating methods; transfer from development lab to QC lab
High ▼

Every analytical method used for batch release and stability testing must be validated per ICH Q2(R2): specificity, linearity, range, accuracy, precision, detection/quantitation limits, and robustness. For stability-indicating methods (HPLC assay and related substances for each API), this is a multi-month analytical chemistry programme. Methods must then be transferred from development laboratory to Truvet QC laboratory with documented method transfer protocols. This is a regulatory prerequisite before any product can be submitted for registration — and it is entirely absent from equipment procurement discussions.

Strategic Context — Why You Should Build This Internally

The MES/SCADA layer is the interface between your physical manufacturing assets and regulated-market access. Without it, your machines produce tablets. With it, your machines produce batch records that FDA, EMA, MHRA, TGA, and institutional acquirers can legally rely on. That difference is your regulatory moat — and it is the primary valuation driver in a pharma M&A transaction or SGX IPO.

Applying your infrastructure model: this system is repeatable (deploys to Malaysia, Philippines, UAE facilities at 30–40% of first-site cost), scalable (zero-per-tag licensing means no incremental software cost as you add machines), and profitable (converts manufacturing CapEx into regulated-market-accessible assets at no marginal cost per batch). Outsourcing this perpetually to a CRO or system integrator is the operational equivalent of renting. The institutional knowledge stays outside the group and you pay each time you need a change.
